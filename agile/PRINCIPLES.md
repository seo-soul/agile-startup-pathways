# Principles behind the Agile Manifesto

The 12 Principles provide operational guidance for living the values of the Manifesto. Read them as a set — they reinforce each other.

---

**1. Customer satisfaction through early, continuous delivery**

> "Our highest priority is to satisfy the customer through early and continuous delivery of valuable software."

Ship early and often. Don't wait for perfect. A working prototype in front of a user tells you more than months of internal design review.

---

**2. Welcome changing requirements**

> "Welcome changing requirements, even late in development. Agile processes harness change for the customer's competitive advantage."

Requirements change because the world changes and because people learn. A process that treats late changes as failures is optimizing for the wrong thing.

---

**3. Deliver working software frequently**

> "Deliver working software frequently, from a couple of weeks to a couple of months, with a preference to the shorter timescale."

Short delivery cycles compress feedback loops. The faster you deliver, the faster you learn what to do next.

---

**4. Business and developers work together daily**

> "Business people and developers must work together daily throughout the project."

Handoffs create waste. Misunderstandings compound. Proximity — physical or virtual — between the people who define value and the people who deliver it accelerates everything.

---

**5. Build around motivated individuals**

> "Build projects around motivated individuals. Give them the environment and support they need, and trust them to get the job done."

Autonomy and trust are prerequisites for high performance. Micromanagement optimizes for compliance, not output.

---

**6. Face-to-face conversation is the most effective communication**

> "The most efficient and effective method of conveying information to and within a development team is face-to-face conversation."

Synchronous, high-bandwidth communication beats documentation for complex problems. Written communication is valuable for decisions and records — not for working through ambiguity in real time.

---

**7. Working software is the primary measure of progress**

> "Working software is the primary measure of progress."

Not stories completed. Not lines of code. Not percentage complete. Running software that delivers value is the only measure that matters.

---

**8. Sustainable development pace**

> "Agile processes promote sustainable development. The sponsors, developers, and users should be able to maintain a constant pace indefinitely."

Heroics are a sign of a broken system, not a solution. A team that burns out produces bugs, attrition, and technical debt. Sustainable pace is a professional standard, not a comfort preference.

---

**9. Continuous attention to technical excellence**

> "Continuous attention to technical excellence and good design enhances agility."

Quality is not a phase. Code quality, test coverage, and clean architecture directly enable the ability to respond to change. Technical debt reduces agility.

---

**10. Simplicity**

> "Simplicity — the art of maximizing the amount of work not done — is essential."

Don't build what isn't needed. Don't over-engineer what is. The best code is often the code that doesn't exist yet. YAGNI (You Aren't Gonna Need It) is a real principle.

---

**11. Self-organizing teams produce the best results**

> "The best architectures, requirements, and designs emerge from self-organizing teams."

Top-down design has limits. The people doing the work have information and context that no architect working in isolation can replicate. Give teams real ownership.

---

**12. Regular reflection and adaptation**

> "At regular intervals, the team reflects on how to become more effective, then tunes and adjusts its behavior accordingly."

The retrospective is not a ceremony — it's a commitment to treating the team's own process as a product to be continuously improved.
