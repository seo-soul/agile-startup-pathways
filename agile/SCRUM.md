# Scrum

Scrum is the most widely used agile framework. It provides a lightweight structure for teams to deliver value iteratively and to improve their process continuously.

> "Scrum is a framework within which people can address complex adaptive problems, while productively and creatively delivering products of the highest possible value."
> — The Scrum Guide

---

## The Three Pillars

Scrum is built on empirical process control — the idea that knowledge comes from experience and that decisions should be based on observed evidence.

| Pillar | What it means |
|--------|---------------|
| **Transparency** | The process and work must be visible to everyone responsible for the outcome |
| **Inspection** | Frequent inspection of progress toward goals to detect problems early |
| **Adaptation** | Adjusting the process or product when inspection reveals deviations |

---

## The Five Values

Teams that embody these values make the three pillars real:

- **Commitment** — Team members personally commit to achieving the sprint goal
- **Courage** — Do the right thing and work on hard problems
- **Focus** — Work on sprint commitments, not everything at once
- **Openness** — Be open about work and challenges with each other and stakeholders
- **Respect** — Team members respect each other as capable, independent people

## Roles

### Product Owner
Responsible for maximizing product value by managing the product backlog. Makes decisions about what to build and in what order. The voice of the customer inside the team.

**Key responsibilities:**
- Maintain and order the product backlog
- Ensure backlog items are clear and understood
- Accept or reject sprint output

### Scrum Master
A servant-leader who helps the team apply Scrum effectively. Removes impediments, facilitates ceremonies, and coaches both the team and the organization.

**Key responsibilities:**
- Facilitate Scrum events
- Remove blockers the team can't remove themselves
- Shield the team from external distractions
- Help the organization understand Scrum

### Developers
The people who do the work — design, code, test, deploy. Cross-functional and self-organizing. Collectively responsible for delivering the sprint increment.

**Key responsibilities:**
- Plan the sprint
- Hold each other accountable to the sprint goal
- Maintain the Definition of Done
- Adapt the plan each day toward the sprint goal

---

## Artifacts

### Product Backlog
An ordered list of everything that might be needed in the product. Continuously refined. Owned by the Product Owner.

### Sprint Backlog
The sprint goal, the selected product backlog items, and a plan for how to deliver them. Created by the Developers during sprint planning.

### Increment
The sum of all completed product backlog items at the end of a sprint. Must be in a usable, releasable state regardless of whether the PO chooses to release it.

## Common Pitfalls

**Scrum-but** — Using most of Scrum but skipping the parts that feel uncomfortable (e.g., "We do Scrum, but we don't do retros"). Usually a sign the team hasn't committed to the process.

**Velocity as a target** — Velocity is a planning tool, not a performance metric. Pressure to increase velocity leads to inflated estimates and degraded quality.

**No sprint goal** — A sprint without a coherent goal is just a list of tasks. The goal gives the team latitude to adapt mid-sprint if something unexpected comes up.

**PO not available** — If the Product Owner can't answer questions during the sprint, work stalls and assumptions multiply. PO availability is critical.
