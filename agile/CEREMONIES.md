# Agile Ceremonies

Ceremonies (also called events or rituals) are the structured touchpoints that give an agile team its rhythm. Each one has a specific purpose — they are not status meetings dressed up with new names.

This guide covers the standard Scrum events. Your team may adapt timing, format, or frequency based on what works for you.

---

## Sprint Planning

**When:** Start of each sprint
**Duration:** Up to 4 hours for a 2-week sprint (scale proportionally)
**Attendees:** All team members

### Purpose

Align the team on what will be worked on during the sprint and how the work will be approached.

### What happens

1. **Set the sprint goal** — The Product Owner proposes a goal; the team collaborates to refine it. The goal should be specific enough to guide decisions but flexible enough to allow adaptation.

2. **Select backlog items** — The team pulls items from the top of the product backlog that they believe they can complete while achieving the sprint goal. The PO answers questions; the team makes commitments.

3. **Create the plan** — Developers decompose selected items into tasks and identify dependencies. The sprint backlog takes shape.

### Signs of a healthy sprint planning

- The team leaves with a clear, shared sprint goal
- Work selected is realistic given known capacity
- Questions to the PO are resolved, not deferred
- The team commits as a group, not as individuals

## Daily Scrum (Standup)

**When:** Every working day, same time and place
**Duration:** 15 minutes (hard cap)
**Attendees:** Developers (PO and SM attend as observers unless they're also Developers)

### Purpose

Inspect progress toward the sprint goal and adapt the daily plan. Not a status report to management.

### Format

The classic three-question format works, but the goal matters more than the format:

- What did I complete yesterday that contributes to the sprint goal?
- What will I do today toward the sprint goal?
- Is there anything blocking my progress?

Some teams use a board walk instead — moving through each in-progress item and discussing it.

### Norms

- Start on time, always
- Keep it to 15 minutes — move conversations that need more time to after
- If you're stuck, say so — blockers fester when unspoken
- The SM helps remove blockers; the standup is not where they get solved

## Sprint Review

**When:** End of each sprint
**Duration:** Up to 2 hours for a 2-week sprint
**Attendees:** Team + stakeholders

### Purpose

Inspect the sprint increment and adapt the product backlog based on what was learned. This is a working session, not a demo theater.

### What happens

1. **Present the increment** — The team demonstrates what was completed during the sprint. Only work that meets the Definition of Done is shown.

2. **Gather feedback** — Stakeholders interact with the product. Questions are encouraged. The PO facilitates discussion about what to do next.

3. **Update the backlog** — Based on feedback and new information, the PO updates priorities. The team discusses what this means for upcoming sprints.

### Signs of a healthy sprint review

- Real stakeholders are present, not just proxies
- Work shown actually runs; no slide decks substituting for software
- Feedback influences the backlog
- It's a dialogue, not a presentation

## Sprint Retrospective

**When:** After the sprint review, before the next sprint planning
**Duration:** Up to 1.5 hours for a 2-week sprint
**Attendees:** The team (SM facilitates; PO may attend if the team is comfortable)

### Purpose

Reflect on the team's process and relationships. Identify what went well, what didn't, and commit to at least one concrete improvement.

### Common formats

**Start / Stop / Continue**
- What should we start doing?
- What should we stop doing?
- What should we continue doing?

**4Ls**
- Liked
- Learned
- Lacked
- Longed for

**Mad / Sad / Glad**
Good for surfacing emotional context alongside process issues.

### Making retros effective

- Create psychological safety — people won't surface real problems in an unsafe environment
- Focus on the system, not individuals — "our process allowed this" vs. "you did this wrong"
- Commit to one concrete action item per retro, not a list of twelve
- Follow up on the previous retro's action item at the start of the next one

## Backlog Refinement

**When:** Mid-sprint, recurring (not an official Scrum event but universally adopted)
**Duration:** 1–2 hours per sprint
**Attendees:** PO + Developers (SM as needed)

### Purpose

Keep the product backlog in a ready state so sprint planning isn't a scramble. Items coming up in the next 1–2 sprints should be understood, sized, and ready to pull.

### What happens

- Review and clarify upcoming stories
- Identify and resolve open questions
- Estimate items (story points, t-shirt sizes, or whatever the team uses)
- Split large items into smaller, sprint-sized chunks
- Reprioritize based on new information

### Signs of a healthy backlog

- The top 2 sprints of work are refined and estimated
- Stories have acceptance criteria
- Dependencies are identified and flagged
- The PO can answer questions without leaving the room

## Ceremony Health Check

If any of these feel like a waste of time, that's signal — not just noise. The ceremony itself usually isn't the problem. Common root causes:

| Symptom | Likely cause |
|---------|-------------|
| Standups run long | People giving status reports instead of flagging blockers |
| Sprint planning is chaotic | Backlog isn't refined; PO unavailable |
| Reviews have no stakeholders | Wrong people were invited; team doesn't control demo timing |
| Retros produce no change | Actions aren't tracked; team doesn't feel safe |
| Refinement skipped | "We'll figure it out in planning" — and then planning takes 6 hours |
